# Stewardship Writing Guide

## Purpose

The Stewardship Framework is a practical guide to responsible care of material life. It extends Ethosism into money, property, body, home, tools, resources, consumption, land, waste, enterprise, infrastructure, inheritance, and care for the material world.

The book should help a reader answer: what has come into my care, what does reality require of it, who is affected by how I use it, and what condition will I leave it in?

## Core Commitments

Every chapter should connect back to five commitments:

1. Reality: material life has limits, costs, decay, scarcity, dependency, and consequences.
2. Care: what enters our custody should be maintained, repaired, used wisely, and protected from neglect.
3. Provision: resources should support responsible life, not vanity, hoarding, waste, or domination.
4. Reciprocity: ownership and use must account for neighbors, workers, dependents, the vulnerable, and future generations.
5. Inheritance: the material world should be handed on in a more truthful and defensible condition.

## Moral Method

Ground claims in five checks:

1. Objective reality: what does this material pattern actually produce in health, debt, resilience, waste, dependency, dignity, and repair?
2. Reciprocity: would the standard remain fair if you were the owner, worker, tenant, lender, borrower, neighbor, child, elder, dependent, poor person, or future inheritor?
3. Integrity: do spending, ownership, maintenance, generosity, consumption, and public claims align?
4. Repair: what happens when waste, debt, neglect, exploitation, damage, hoarding, or fragility appears?
5. Long-term responsibility: what does this pattern become across years, decades, or generations?

Do not treat stewardship as mere budgeting, minimalism, wealth accumulation, environmental signaling, or private lifestyle. The standard is responsible custody of material reality.

## Theology And Politics Stance

The Stewardship Framework is secular and non-theological. It should be usable by atheists, agnostics, and religious readers.

The book may discuss money, poverty, property, enterprise, ecology, inheritance, debt, public goods, and household life, but it should not become a partisan program. It may name real failure, waste, exploitation, hoarding, debt bondage, ecological damage, vanity consumption, institutional neglect, and public irresponsibility, but it should reason from consequences, reciprocity, integrity, repair, and inheritance rather than party identity or culture-war signaling.

## Voice

Write in a direct essay style. The tone should be serious, practical, and morally clear without becoming theatrical, sermon-like, scolding, prosperity-preaching, ascetic for its own sake, bureaucratic, or ideologically smug.

Prefer:

- Concrete claims.
- Plain language.
- Clear distinctions between provision, hoarding, generosity, waste, ownership, and custody.
- Maintenance over image.
- Repair over disposal where repair is reasonable.
- Generosity without irresponsibility.
- Prudence without fear.
- Nuance without evasion.

Avoid:

- Personal finance hacks.
- Anti-money resentment.
- Prosperity ideology.
- Lifestyle minimalism as status.
- Environmental theater.
- Body optimization culture.
- Culture-war shorthand.
- Partisan signaling.
- Academic throat-clearing.
- Overstated certainty where evidence varies by place, income, family, disability, climate, law, and season.

## Chapter Pattern

Most chapters should follow this shape:

1. Define the material domain and why it matters.
2. Identify the common failure mode.
3. State the Stewardship standard for that domain.
4. Show how the standard follows from reality, care, provision, reciprocity, repair, and inheritance.
5. Address tradeoffs, edge cases, or predictable objections.
6. End with a concise behavioral expectation or decision standard.

Do not force this pattern when a chapter needs a different structure, but keep the chapter moving from principle to practical judgment.

## Practice Blocks

Domain chapters should leave the reader with a usable practice. Include a short practice section or equivalent closing application that covers:

1. Plain standard: what the chapter asks the reader to take seriously.
2. Reality test: what this material pattern actually produces.
3. Care test: what is being maintained, repaired, neglected, or consumed.
4. Reciprocity test: what role reversal requires from the person with more ownership, money, leverage, or custody.
5. Provision test: whether resources are supporting responsible life.
6. Repair test: what waste, damage, debt, exploitation, neglect, or disorder needs correction.
7. Long-term test: what this pattern becomes across years, decades, or generations.
8. First practice: one concrete behavior the reader can change this week.

The practice should make the essay actionable without turning stewardship into a checklist for its own sake.

## Consistency Checks

Before finishing a chapter or revision, check:

- Does the chapter depend on theology, partisan identity, prosperity ideology, anti-wealth resentment, or lifestyle status? If so, rewrite it in secular, nonpartisan terms.
- Does it explain why the stewardship standard is good, not merely assert it?
- Does it respect role reversal for owners, workers, borrowers, lenders, tenants, neighbors, dependents, and future generations?
- Does it account for limits, income variation, disability, place, law, risk, hidden costs, and predictable consequences?
- Does it distinguish stewardship from hoarding, vanity, waste, self-punishing simplicity, and irresponsible generosity?
- Does it connect to care, provision, reciprocity, repair, and inheritance?
- Does it avoid excusing exploitation, neglect, debt evasion, waste, domination, fear, greed, or public abdication?
- Does it sound like Project Creed prose: clear, sober, concrete, and adult?
